Surgical Scheduling Dashboard

A dashboard for optimizing surgery schedules and enhancing the process of information processing for surgical booking

Overview

This case study documents the transformation of a complex healthcare scheduling application‚Äîfrom an over-engineered, untested prototype to a streamlined, user-centered design system. I worked on the Surgical OR Scheduling System for ORCHID Analytics, developing a GUI for a children's hospital in North America to help administrators model "what-if" scenarios for surgical capacity planning.

The hospital was struggling with surgical scheduling challenges: manual processes, communication gaps between teams, and optimization algorithm outputs that were too complex for daily users. As the sole UX researcher and designer for 17 months, I led the research, testing, and redesign efforts‚Äîlearning critical lessons about design systems, user validation, and AI-assisted development along the way.

Key Achievement: Reduced design iteration time by 70% while improving consistency and usability by establishing a design system first, then building features‚Äîthe opposite approach from my original attempt.

The Problem Space

Healthcare Context

Hospital operating rooms are complex environments where 14-16 OR rooms operate daily across multiple surgical services. Surgeons, staff, and patients must be coordinated efficiently through block schedules that allocate time blocks (6.5-10.5 hours) to different surgical departments. Effective waitlist management directly impacts patient outcomes and hospital revenue.

Business Challenge

Hospital administrators needed a system to:

  • Visualize current OR block allocations across services (Plastics, Ortho, ENT, etc.)
  • Model "what-if" scenarios (e.g., "What if we add weekend OR hours?")
  • Track KPIs: Waitlist Reduction, Overtime Hours, Bed Occupancy, QBP Funding, Staffing Requirements
  • Drill down from high-level OR grids to surgeon schedules to individual patient allocations

Key User Insights

Complex Decision Matrix: "Division Managers juggle multiple factors: surgeon availability, equipment resources, post-op capacity, and HR constraints - all requiring real-time visibility."

Data Overwhelm: "Existing systems presented too much information without prioritization, making it difficult to focus on critical decisions that impact patient care."

Manual Processes: "Teams wanted to upload existing scheduling files rather than re-inputting data, and needed clear visualization of schedule changes."

Time-Sensitive Planning: "Users needed to monitor and optimize schedules for the next two weeks while running scenario planning for better resource allocation."

How I Approached the Research

I started by surveying the two user groups to understand what they actually needed from a dashboard. I wanted to know: what decisions are you making, what information do you need, and what's currently frustrating about the process?

Research Questions & Findings

What I Wanted to Learn

  • What decisions do Division Managers make daily?
  • What information is most critical vs. nice-to-have?
  • What are the biggest pain points in current workflows?
  • How do they know when something needs immediate attention?

What Division Managers Actually Do

  • Allocate surgery time blocks for their departments
  • Coordinate with multiple teams inside and outside their department
  • Consider surgeon schedules, equipment availability, HR capacity, and post-op bed availability all at once

Critical Mistakes I Made (and What I Learned)

Mistake #1: Design Before Testing

What went wrong: I created 3 different navigation versions without user validation. I spent weeks perfecting visual design, colors, and layouts, building complex drill-down flows without testing if users even needed this hierarchy.

Result: Wasted 6+ weeks on features that might not solve the actual user problem.

Lesson learned: Build minimum viable prototypes first, test with 3-5 users, iterate based on feedback, and polish AFTER validation.

Mistake #2: No Design System

What went wrong: Changed top navigation bar design multiple times, had no color palette established upfront, action buttons had inconsistent colors, and kept "experimenting" with styling during feature development.

Result: The UI felt disjointed, and I kept redesigning the same components over and over.

Lesson learned: Establish design system upfront (color scheme, typography, reusable components, spacing standards) before building features.

The Redesign Approach: AI-Assisted Development

When I revisited this project in 2024-2025, I took a completely different approach. By establishing a design system first and leveraging AI assistance, I reduced design iteration time by 70% while improving consistency.

How AI Accelerated Development

  • Rapid Prototyping: Generated React component boilerplate in seconds following established patterns
  • Data Structure Design: Used AI to analyze hospital Excel files and generate data models and relationships
  • Context Management: Built ScenarioContext and AllocationContext with AI assistance, avoiding prop-drilling issues
  • Consistent Styling: Once design system was defined, AI generated CSS that followed the rules‚Äîno more experimenting

Important Note: AI didn't replace my design thinking‚Äîit amplified it by removing tedious tasks (code boilerplate, data parsing, styling consistency) so I could focus on understanding users, defining problems, and validating solutions.

Reflection: What This Project Taught Me

This project was a humbling lesson in the difference between design and decoration.

Three Years Ago, I Thought "Good Design" Meant:

  • Perfectly aligned pixels
  • Beautiful color palettes
  • Smooth animations
  • Impressive technical features

Now I Know "Good Design" Means:

  • Solving real user problems
  • Testing assumptions early
  • Establishing systems before details
  • Knowing when to stop designing and start validating

The Meta-Lesson: Designing the Design Process

This case study isn't just about a scheduling app. It's about how I design now:

Old Process:

Idea ‚Üí Build ‚Üí Polish ‚Üí (Maybe test?) ‚Üí Realize it's wrong ‚Üí Start over

New Process:

Problem ‚Üí Research ‚Üí Low-fi prototype ‚Üí Test ‚Üí Iterate ‚Üí Design system ‚Üí Build ‚Üí Test ‚Üí Refine

Key Takeaways

Design Systems Prevent Future Pain

Establish patterns before building features. The upfront investment saves exponential time later.

AI Accelerates Implementation, Not Strategy

Use AI to execute decisions, not to make them. My role is to understand users, define problems, and validate solutions.

Test Early, Design Later

Validation beats perfection. A rough prototype tested with users is worth more than a polished design that nobody needs.

Document Decisions

Creating documentation helped me understand complex data relationships, plan integration, and communicate with stakeholders effectively.

Final Thought: This project showcases my growth as a designer‚Äînot just in technical skills, but in understanding that great design isn't about making things pretty. It's about making things useful, usable, and delightful‚Äîin that order.
